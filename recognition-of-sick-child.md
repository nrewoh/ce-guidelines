# Recognition of a Sick Child (Updated in June 2022)

Early recognition of physiological decompensation in pediatric patients prevents progression to respiratory or cardiac failure.

## 1. The Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)
This rapid, non-invasive assessment takes less than 30 seconds and requires no stethoscope or monitor:

### A. Appearance
* Evaluate muscle tone, interactiveness, consolability, look/gaze, and speech/cry (**TICLS**).
* *Abnormal finding:* Lethargy, poor eye contact, or unresponsiveness indicates central nervous system dysfunction.

### B. Work of Breathing
* Observe for nasal flaring, retractions (subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal), grunting, stridor, wheezing, or tachypnea.
* *Abnormal finding:* Marked accessory muscle use indicates respiratory compensation or impending failure.

### C. Circulation to Skin
* Evaluate for pallor, mottling, cyanosis, or petechiae.
* *Abnormal finding:* Mottled or pale skin reflects poor peripheral perfusion and early shock.

---

## 2. High-Risk Clinical "Red Flags"
If any of these signs are present, prioritize immediate physician involvement:

* **Central Cyanosis:** SpO2 < 92% in room air.
* **Altered Neurology:** AVPU scale reading Anything less than **A**lert (V = Responds to Voice, P = Responds to Pain, U = Unresponsive).
* **Petechial Rash:** Especially when accompanied by a high febrile state (suspected meningococcemia).
* **Extreme Tachycardia/Bradycardia:** Heart rates completely out of normal ranges for the child's age group.
